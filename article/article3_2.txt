As the United States' economic and military power has expanded over the past eighty years, the rate and perceived necessity of emergency has increased. Presidents have often renewed national emergencies enacted by previous executive branches in patterns that are largely nonpartisan. This indicates that parties' foreign policy platforms, specifically regarding economic diplomacy in times of war and unrest, are more similar to one another than not. Unless an emergency is wholly domestic, economic sanctions seem to be the United States' best method of arm twisting. With globalization and increased market interdependency, as a global superpower the United States can often act as the head surgeon, cutting off a country's limbs and sewing them back on as they see fit. But it is imperative, as the United States inserts its interests in various corners of the world, in Palestine, in the Ukraine, in Central America, at its own border, to ask: whose insurance is covering the cutting of the limbs?
